<div class="sec vis" id="s1">
  <div class="sh"><div class="snum">1</div><h2 class="stitle">Discover Hero Carousel — Image 1</h2><span class="sbadge bw">Fix + Elevate</span></div>
  <div class="iq">"The Morocco hero is genuinely beautiful — dark photography, bold Noto Serif, clean gradient, flag emoji. This is what a premium culinary travel app looks like. The problems are not the aesthetic. They're the interaction layer sitting on top of it."</div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fic">!</div><p class="ft">"Let's Go" + side-by-side bookmark = confused double CTA</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 1: Two equal-weight buttons at hero bottom — large white pill "Let's Go" and a ghost circle bookmark icon side by side.</div>
    <p class="fb">Two actions at identical visual prominence create choice paralysis. The user's eye bounces between them — "do I go, or do I save?" This is the exact opposite of what a hero should do: one bold action, one quiet secondary. The bookmark doesn't belong here as a button. It should float in the top-right corner of the hero image — always accessible, never competing.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Fix</div>Move bookmark to a 32×32pt ghost circle top-right of the hero image. Make "Explore Morocco" the sole full-width CTA. One hero, one action. This change alone will meaningfully increase tap-through.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fic">!</div><p class="ft">"Let's Go" is transactional language on an editorial brand</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 1: Same white pill "Let's Go" regardless of which country is active in the carousel.</div>
    <p class="fb">Your editorial copy says "Tonight, lose yourself in the warmth of Marrakech. Tagine, mint tea, and stories from the medina." — and then the CTA says "Let's Go." That's a Lyft button. Every other word on this screen is magazine-quality writing. The Seasonal Picks card (Image 7) uses "Read the Collection →" — that's the voice this brand should use everywhere.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Fix — 30 min</div>Dynamic CTA: <code>"Explore " + country.name</code> → "Explore Morocco", "Explore Japan", "Explore Italy". Country-specific, editorial-toned, and costs nothing to build.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fiw">~</div><p class="ft">8 pagination dots for 6 countries — data mismatch</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 1: 8 white dots in the pagination row. Spec lists 6 countries.</div>
    <p class="fb">If there are 6 countries, there should be 6 dots. 8 suggests duplicated slides or ghost entries in the carousel. A user who sees 8 dots will swipe to all 8 and hit confusing repetition — breaking trust in the data integrity of the app.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Audit</div>Check carousel data source — ensure dot count exactly matches country array length. The active terracotta pill dot is well-designed; just fix the total.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fig">✓</div><p class="ft">Hero photography, Noto Serif, gradient treatment — genuinely premium</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 1: Dark moody tagine photo, bold "Morocco" serif headline, editorial subtext, terracotta active dot, flag emoji.</div>
    <p class="fb">This is competing with Airbnb's editorial destination cards. The photography choice, typeface pairing, gradient legibility treatment — all correct. The flag emoji next to the headline is smart: informational and personality-adding simultaneously.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Enhance</div>Add a 0.3× parallax on the hero image as the user scrolls — the image drifts slightly upward while the text scrolls at normal speed. Ten lines of code, immediate premium feel. Every top travel app does this.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fiw">~</div><p class="ft">Destination ring labels are ALL CAPS — less refined than they should be</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 1: JAPAN / MOROCCO / MEXICO / INDIA below the circle images.</div>
    <p class="fb">Apple HIG recommends sentence case for navigation labels. ALL CAPS reads as shouting and feels slightly utilitarian — at odds with the editorial premium positioning. The active ring (terracotta border on Morocco) is correct and well-executed. Just sentence-case the labels.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Quick fix — 5 min</div>Japan, Morocco, Mexico, India. Title Case. One constant change in the destination ring component.</div>
  </div>
</div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fig">✓</div><p class="ft">"Tonight's Plan" + recipe card + "Start Cooking" terracotta CTA — excellent UX</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 2: White card with recipe photo, Japan flag, "Quick" badge, bold recipe title, description, prominent terracotta "Start Cooking" button.</div>
    <p class="fb">The "Quick" tag badge is perfect progressive disclosure — the user knows the time commitment before they commit. "Start Cooking" in terracotta is the strongest CTA in the entire app — action-specific, brand-colored, impossible to miss. This card does everything right.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Enhance</div>Make this card time-aware using the device clock: "Tonight's Plan" → "This Weekend" (Fri/Sat), "Quick Lunch" (11am–1pm). Zero user data needed, but makes the app feel like it knows the user's context.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fic">!</div><p class="ft">Featured Locations cards are solid gray — broken image states</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 2: Two "Featured Locations" cards at bottom — both completely gray with barely-visible text "Marrakech" and "Chefchaouen".</div>
    <p class="fb">These are broken or unloaded images. For a visually-led premium app, this is the worst possible impression of "Featured Locations" — the section title promises richness and gray boxes deliver nothing. If images are loading slowly, use a skeleton shimmer. If they're missing from the seed, placeholder them with a terracotta gradient + city name. Never show gray to the user.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Critical fix</div>Implement skeleton shimmer cards while images load. Ensure Unsplash ONBOARDING_IMAGES URLs are seeded correctly for featured cities. Add a terracotta gradient fallback if the image URL fails to load.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fiw">~</div><p class="ft">"New: 🇮🇹 Italy" chip border creates visual inconsistency with other cuisine chips</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 2: Italy and Japan chips have a darker/terracotta border. Morocco, Mexico, India, Thailand chips are plain light gray.</div>
    <p class="fb">The differentiation is intentional and communicates "new" — but the heavy border color change is a blunt instrument. It disrupts the visual rhythm of the chip row and draws too much attention relative to the content sections below.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Refine</div>Replace the dark border treatment with a small "NEW" dot badge on the flag emoji, or a tiny terracotta pill label. Same information, less visual interruption.</div>
  </div>

<div class="sec" id="s3">
  <div class="sh"><div class="snum">3</div><h2 class="stitle">Tonight's Tasting Menu + Cultural Quote — Image 3</h2><span class="sbadge bg">App's Signature Feature</span></div>
  <div class="iq">"'Tonight's Tasting Menu' organized as Soup → Main Course → Dessert is the single most differentiated UX pattern in this app. No other culinary app does this. This is Fork & Compass's signature — the thing that makes it irreplaceable."</div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fig">✓</div><p class="ft">Course structure (Soup / Main Course / Dessert) is a genuine product innovation</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 3: SOUP / MAIN COURSE / DESSERT terracotta headers, each with thumbnail + title + description + chevron in a card.</div>
    <p class="fb">This frames cooking as a dining experience, not a recipe lookup. It mirrors how a real Moroccan feast unfolds sequentially. The terracotta course labels match the system perfectly. The chevron arrows on each card correctly signal navigation. This is the feature worth putting in press materials.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Protect and extend</div>Never simplify this into a flat list. Extend it: add "Amuse-Bouche" or "Aperitif" for Italian, "Banchan" for Korean, "Mezze" for Middle Eastern. Let each cuisine's course structure reflect its culinary culture.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fic">!</div><p class="ft">Recipe titles truncated mid-word across all three course cards</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 3: "Hearty Moroccan Lamb and Pearl Barl..." / "Moroccan Lamb Tagine with Preserve..." / "Mini Moroccan Dried Fruit and Almond Pa..."</div>
    <p class="fb">"Barl..." instead of "Barley", "Pa..." instead of "Pastry" — the truncation cuts mid-word because the character limit is hitting in the middle of a word. This looks like a layout bug, not an intentional design choice. It breaks the editorial credibility of a premium product.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Fix everywhere</div>Set <code>numberOfLines={2}</code> on title Text components and use <code>ellipsizeMode="tail"</code> — React Native will truncate at word boundaries, never mid-character. Apply this fix to every card type in the app: Tasting Menu, Street Food, Related Stories, Heritage Spices.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fig">✓</div><p class="ft">Cultural pull quote is emotionally the best content in the app</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 3: Cream card, chat bubble icon, serif quote: "In Morocco, a guest is a gift. We do not just share a meal; we share our history, our warmth, and our home through the art of the spice."</div>
    <p class="fb">This single card does more for brand storytelling than any UI element. It contextualizes food within culture — exactly what Fork & Compass promises. The cream background, serif treatment, and chat bubble icon all land correctly.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Enhance</div>Add a real attribution — a Moroccan chef, food writer, or cultural figure. Authenticity in the attribution makes the quote 10× more powerful. Also consider making quotes swipeable: one per cultural theme (hospitality / spice / ceremony / community).</div>
  </div>

<div class="sec" id="s4">
  <div class="sh"><div class="snum">4</div><h2 class="stitle">The Spice Market + Cultural Etiquette — Image 4</h2><span class="sbadge bg">Best Content, One Bug</span></div>
  <div class="iq">"'The Spice Market' and 'Cultural Etiquette' are magazine-quality editorial design on mobile. This section is the product vision fully realized. One data bug ruins it."</div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fic">!</div><p class="ft">Same two images repeat for all four spice grid items — credibility-breaking</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 4: Row 1 — alley photo + tagine photo. Row 2 — identical alley photo + identical tagine photo.</div>
    <p class="fb">This is a seed data issue. The spice grid uses placeholder images, and there are only 2 unique images cycling through 4 slots. For a visually-led premium app, seeing the same image twice in a 4-item grid immediately signals "template, not content." A user's trust in the app's authenticity evaporates in this moment.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Critical fix</div>Source 4 distinct images for Morocco spices: saffron threads, whole cumin seeds, ras el hanout blend, dried paprika flakes. Seed individually. Image uniqueness is non-negotiable for a content-first app. Until real images are available, 4 different Unsplash query URLs will work.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fig">✓</div><p class="ft">Spice descriptions are editorial-quality writing</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 4: "Hand-picked from the Taliouine region." / "Earthiness central to every tagine." / '"Head of the shop" — a complex blend.' / "Sun-dried for vibrant color and depth."</div>
    <p class="fb">These one-line descriptions are doing the work of an encyclopedia entry compressed into a tweet. "Earthiness central to every tagine" — that's a flavor profile, a cultural context, and a culinary cue in 5 words. This writing quality is what separates Fork & Compass from every recipe app that just lists ingredient names.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Protect</div>Make every spice card tappable. The editorial quality of the descriptions justifies a full spice detail view — expanded description, cultural origin, recipes that use it. Add a visible chevron or subtle border affordance so users know these cards navigate somewhere.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="f">
    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fig">✓</div><p class="ft">Cultural Etiquette with icon circles is pedagogically excellent</p></div>
    <div class="ev"><div class="evl">Seen on screenshot</div>Image 4: Cream icon circles with symbols, "THE RIGHT HAND" / "THE TEA CEREMONY" headers, explanatory text.</div>
    <p class="fb">Icon → concept name → explanation is the same three-part structure Apple uses in feature onboarding. It's proven to aid retention. "Mint tea is a sign of hospitality. It is poured from a height to create a crown of foam — never refuse a first cup." — that sentence makes the reader want to be in Morocco.</p>
    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Minor fix</div>Change "THE RIGHT HAND" / "THE TEA CEREMONY" from ALL CAPS to Title Case. Ensure 80pt bottom padding so content doesn't collide with the tab bar chrome.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="sh"><div class="snum">5</div><h2 class="stitle">Cultural Etiquette cont. + Heritage Spices — Image 5</h2><span class="sbadge bw">Strong, Check Safe Area</span></div>

    <div class="fh"><div class="fi fig">✓</div><p class="ft">Heritage Spices horizontal scroll cards are premium editorial format</p></div>

    <div class="fr"><div class="frl">Make tappable + tag interactive</div>The terracotta tag ("SIGNATURE BLEND") should filter recipes by this spice when tapped — transforming a label into a discovery mechanic. Add a chevron to the card to signal it navigates to a spice detail view.</div>
