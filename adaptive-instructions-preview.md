# Adaptive Instruction Variants — Sample Preview

Each recipe step has three versions:
- **Standard** — the default instruction
- **First Steps** — encouraging, explains WHY, defines unfamiliar tools/terms
- **Chef's Table** — terse, precise, chef vocabulary

---

## 1. Classic Garlic Bread — Step 2 (Making the butter)

**Standard**
> In a small bowl, combine the softened butter, minced garlic, chopped parsley, and a pinch of salt. Mix with a fork until fully incorporated.

**First Steps**
> In a bowl, mix the softened butter with the garlic, parsley, and a pinch of salt. "Softened" means the butter should feel like playdough — not melted, not cold and hard. If yours is still cold, microwave it in 5-second bursts until it gives when you press it. Mash and stir with a fork until everything is evenly combined.

**Chef's Table**
> Compound butter: ensure fat is at true plasticity — 18 to 20°C. Cold butter won't absorb the garlic evenly; melted butter will separate on contact with the bread. Mix until homogeneous.

## 2. Classic Garlic Bread — Step 4 (Broiling)

**Standard**
> Switch the oven to the broil setting and broil the bread for 2 to 3 minutes, watching carefully until the top is deeply golden and the edges are beginning to crisp.

**First Steps**
> Switch your oven to "broil" — this turns on only the top heating element and works like a very intense overhead grill. The bread will go from pale to perfectly golden very quickly, so don't walk away. Open the oven door every 30 seconds to check. You want deep golden-brown colour on the surface with slightly crisped edges.

**Chef's Table**
> Broil at maximum element output. Colour develops rapidly via Maillard reaction at this proximity — watch constantly, 2 minutes is typically sufficient. Target: deep amber crust, not pale gold.

## 3. Individual Tiramisu — Step 2 (Whisking yolks)

**Standard**
> In a mixing bowl, vigorously whisk the egg yolks and sugar together until the mixture triples in volume, becomes thick, and turns a pale yellow color.

**First Steps**
> Whisk the egg yolks and sugar together energetically — use an electric mixer if you have one, or a balloon whisk. You're working until the mixture turns pale yellow, becomes thick like heavy cream, and nearly triples in size. This takes 3 to 4 minutes of vigorous whisking and is the key to a light, airy tiramisu.

**Chef's Table**
> Ribbon to full zabaglione consistency — pale, triple volume, trailing ribbon that holds for 3 seconds. This air incorporation is the structural foundation of the cream. Under-whisking produces a dense, flat tiramisu.

## 4. Minestrone — Step 3 (Blooming spices)

**Standard**
> Add the garlic, tomato paste, Italian seasoning, and red pepper flakes to the vegetables. Cook for 2 minutes, stirring constantly, until the tomato paste darkens slightly.

**First Steps**
> Add the garlic, tomato paste, Italian seasoning, and pepper flakes directly to the vegetables in the pot. Stir and cook for 2 minutes. You'll notice the tomato paste going from bright orange-red to a darker, almost brick-red colour — that's exactly what you want. This process deepens the flavour significantly.

**Chef's Table**
> Toast the tomato paste in the residual fat — the sugars caramelise and the water content evaporates, concentrating the glutamates and producing a deeper, roasted tomato flavour. 2 minutes minimum; the paste should turn dark amber.

## 5. Panna Cotta — Step 1 (Blooming gelatin)

**Standard**
> Pour the cold milk into a small bowl and sprinkle the gelatin powder evenly over the surface. Let it sit undisturbed for 5 to 10 minutes to bloom and hydrate.

**First Steps**
> Pour the cold milk into a small bowl. Sprinkle the gelatin powder slowly and evenly across the surface — don't dump it in a pile. Leave it alone for 5 to 10 minutes. You'll watch the gelatin absorb the milk and become a soft, spongy mass. This process is called blooming and it's essential for smooth gelatin.

**Chef's Table**
> Bloom the gelatin in cold liquid — sprinkling evenly prevents clumping. The hydration phase ensures full granule saturation; bloomed gelatin dissolves instantly in hot cream without lumping.

## 6. Italian Roasted Peppers — Step 3 (Steaming)

**Standard**
> Using tongs, immediately transfer the hot peppers to a mixing bowl. Cover tightly with plastic wrap or a flat plate and let them sit for 15 minutes. This creates steam that loosens the skins.

**First Steps**
> As soon as the peppers come out of the oven, use tongs to move them into a bowl. Immediately cover the bowl tightly with cling film or a flat plate and leave for 15 minutes. The trapped steam loosens the charred skin from the flesh, making peeling effortless.

**Chef's Table**
> Steam in a sealed vessel immediately post-roast — the trapped steam creates a micro-environment that separates the epidermis from the flesh. 15 minutes minimum; longer is fine. Do not run under cold water — this dilutes the flesh.

## 7. White Bean Caprese — Step 4 (Finishing)

**Standard**
> Drizzle the balsamic vinegar over the salad and top with the remaining basil and a pinch of flaky salt.

**First Steps**
> Drizzle the balsamic vinegar across the salad as the final flavour layer — its sweet acidity brightens everything. Scatter the remaining torn basil leaves on top and finish with a pinch of flaky salt right before serving.

**Chef's Table**
> Balsamic last — adding acid too early causes the mozzarella to tighten and the basil to darken. Flaky salt at service: textural contrast and a burst of pure salinity on the first bite. Serve within 15 minutes of dressing.

## 8. Soba Noodle Salad — Step 2 (Cold rinse)

**Standard**
> Drain the noodles in a colander and immediately rinse them vigorously under cold running water until the water runs clear and the noodles are fully chilled.

**First Steps**
> Drain the noodles immediately into a colander and run cold tap water over them aggressively, tossing them with your hand. Keep rinsing until the water runs clear and the noodles are completely cold. This stops the cooking, removes excess starch, and makes the texture springy instead of sticky.

**Chef's Table**
> Cold rinse is essential for soba — it halts cooking, removes surface starch that would cause clumping, and sets the characteristic springy QQ texture. Rinse until effluent runs completely clear.

## 9. Miso Mayonnaise — Step 2 (Pre-hydrating miso)

**Standard**
> Whisk the miso and vinegar together until perfectly smooth. Breaking down the miso with a liquid first prevents stubborn lumps from forming in the mayonnaise.

**First Steps**
> Whisk the miso and vinegar together until the mixture is completely smooth — no lumps anywhere. This step of diluting the miso in a small amount of liquid before adding the mayo is the secret to a silky final sauce.

**Chef's Table**
> Pre-hydration of the miso with a small volume of liquid creates a slurry that integrates cleanly into the emulsified mayonnaise. Attempting to combine miso directly with mayo creates lumps that persist even with vigorous whisking.

## 10. Tori Soba — Step 3 (Poaching chicken)

**Standard**
> Carefully add the bite-sized chicken pieces to the simmering broth. Poach gently until the chicken is tender, completely opaque throughout, and reaches an internal temperature of 74C/165F. Skim off any grayish foam.

**First Steps**
> Gently lower the chicken pieces into the simmering broth. Don't let the heat get too high — you want a gentle simmer, not a rolling boil, which would toughen the chicken. As the chicken cooks, you'll notice a grey, foamy scum rising to the surface. Skim this off with a spoon and discard it. The chicken is done when it's fully opaque with no pink inside.

**Chef's Table**
> Gentle poach at 80 to 85°C produces tender, moist chicken. A rolling boil at 100°C denatures the proteins aggressively and results in tough, dry meat. Skim the albumin foam immediately — it clouds the broth and creates an unclean, murky appearance.

## 11. Coffee Jelly — Step 6 (Assembly)

**Standard**
> Spoon the freshly whipped salted caramel cream generously over the chilled coffee jelly. Garnish with a tiny pinch of flaky sea salt if desired, and serve immediately.

**First Steps**
> Spoon a generous layer of the caramel cream over each glass of coffee jelly. Add just a tiny pinch of flaky sea salt on top — it enhances the sweetness and adds a subtle crunch. Serve immediately and don't let it sit — the cream will melt into the jelly.

**Chef's Table**
> Service is immediate — the cream begins to warm and lose structure within minutes. Flaky salt at the table surface only, not mixed in, for textural contrast. The bitter coffee jelly and sweet salted cream is a classic bitter-sweet-salt interplay.

## 12. Vegetarian Miso Soup — Step 5 (Adding miso)

**Standard**
> Turn off the heat completely. Lower a fine mesh strainer slightly into the hot broth, add the miso paste into the strainer, and use a spoon or chopsticks to dissolve it seamlessly into the soup.

**First Steps**
> Turn the heat completely off. Place a small strainer in the broth, put the miso paste inside the strainer, and stir it against the mesh with a spoon — the miso dissolves through the strainer and into the soup without any lumps. Never boil miso — it destroys the delicate flavour.

**Chef's Table**
> Miso is never boiled — heat above 60°C denatures the active enzymes and drives off the volatile aromatic compounds responsible for its complex flavour. Dissolve off-heat through a strainer for perfectly smooth, fully flavoured soup.

## 13. Souffle Pancakes — Step 3 (Meringue)

**Standard**
> In a separate, spotless metal or glass mixing bowl, beat the chilled egg whites using an electric hand mixer until frothy. Gradually add the caster sugar in three additions, whipping continuously until stiff, glossy peaks form.

**First Steps**
> This step is critical. Beat the cold egg whites in a completely clean bowl — any trace of fat or yolk will prevent them from whipping. Start at medium speed until foamy, then add the sugar gradually in three portions while beating continuously. Stop when the meringue forms stiff, shiny peaks that hold a sharp point when you lift the beaters.

**Chef's Table**
> Stiff, glossy meringue is the structural backbone of the soufflé pancake. Bowl must be fat-free (wipe with vinegar if uncertain). Add sugar gradually in three additions to build a stable foam. Over-whipped whites become dry and grainy — stop at sharp stiff peaks.

## 14. Korean Army Stew — Step 2 (The arrangement)

**Standard**
> In a shallow wide pot or braiser, arrange the kimchi, spam, hot dogs, ground pork, tofu, onion, scallions, and baked beans in separate neat clusters around the edge. Place the seasoning paste in the center.

**First Steps**
> This is the fun part — lay out all the ingredients in separate clusters around the edges of a wide, shallow pot, like a mosaic. Kimchi here, Spam there, tofu in another spot. Place the seasoning paste in the centre. The beautiful arrangement matters — it's traditionally cooked and served at the table.

**Chef's Table**
> Arranged assembly is traditional and functional — it keeps ingredients distinct during the initial boiling phase so they cook at their optimal rate. The paste in the centre dissolves into the stock from the outside in as the liquid rises.

## 15. Futomaki — Step 13 (Slicing)

**Standard**
> Wipe the blade of a very sharp knife with a damp kitchen towel. Cut the sushi roll in half, then cut each half in half again to create equal quarters, yielding eight pieces per roll. Wipe the blade clean between every single slice.

**First Steps**
> Use the sharpest knife you have. Wet a kitchen towel and keep it nearby. Cut the roll in half first with one confident, single stroke. Then cut each half into quarters. Critically: wipe the blade clean with the damp cloth between every single cut. Rice sticking to the blade tears the nori on the next cut.

**Chef's Table**
> A sharp, wet blade is non-negotiable. Wipe between every slice — rice and gelatin from the tamago accumulate on the blade and drag on subsequent cuts. Single-stroke cuts only; sawing compresses the layers and ruins the cross-section.
